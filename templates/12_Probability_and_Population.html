Population and Sample Overview

Sample:

A sample includes one or more observations drawn from the population, and the measurable characteristic of a sample is a statistic.

Sampling is the process of selecting the sample from the population. For example, some people living in India form a sample of the population.

  • There are 2 types of sampling:
    • Probability sampling
    • Non-probability sampling

Population:

A population includes all the elements from the dataset, and measurable characteristics of the population such as mean and standard deviation are referred to as parameters.

For example, all people living in India indicate the population of India.

  • There are different types of populations:
    • Finite Population
    • Infinite Population
    • Existent Population
    • Hypothetical Population

Probability Theory

Probability Theory is very important to understand statistical inference, which is the foundation of analytical methods. In order to infer from sample data to the population (which is not known), we need to understand probability theory and its application. Since it's not feasible to truly know a population due to its large size and the difficulty in collecting data, we work with samples of data.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It is calculated using the formula:

Probability of event B given event A (ùëÉ(B|A)) = (Probability of both events A and B occurring) / (Probability of event A)

For example:

  • Probability of a ball being dotted given that it is red: ùëÉ(B|A) = ùëÉ(AB) / ùëÉ(A) = 0.1 / 0.4 = 0.25
  • Probability of a ball not being dotted given that it's red: 1 - ùëÉ(B|A) = 1 - (ùëÉ(AB) / ùëÉ(A)) = 1 - (0.1 / 0.4) = 0.75

Simple Probability Problem

Suppose we toss a coin 3 times. What is the probability that we will see more than 1 heads?

Standard Approach:

  1. List all possible outcomes (Sample Space) - B
  2. List the number of outcomes of interest ‚Äî A
  3. Divide A/B ‚Äî probability of outcome

Solution:

1. List all possible outcomes (Sample Space) - B

Since we are tossing a coin 3 times, there are 23 = 8 possible outcomes.

Sample Space (B) = {HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT}

2. List the number of outcomes of interest ‚Äî A

We are interested in the outcomes where we see more than 1 heads.

Outcomes of Interest (A) = {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH} (4 outcomes)

3. Divide A/B ‚Äî probability of outcome

Probability of getting more than 1 heads = P(A) / P(B)

P(A) = 4 (number of outcomes of interest)

P(B) = 8 (total possible outcomes)

Probability = 4 / 8 = 0.5

So, the probability that we will see more than 1 heads when tossing a coin 3 times is 0.5, or 50%.

Probability Problem: Drawing Cards from a Deck

Solution:

Suppose we are drawing one card from a well-shuffled deck of playing cards.

Probability that it will be a black card:

Since there are 26 black cards in a standard deck of 52 cards, the probability of drawing a black card is:

Probability = 26/52 = 1/2

Probability that it will be a heart:

Since there are 13 hearts in a standard deck of 52 cards, the probability of drawing a heart card is:

Probability = 13/52 = 1/4

Probability that it will be a queen of hearts:

Since there is 1 queen of hearts in a standard deck of 52 cards, the probability of drawing the queen of hearts is:

Probability = 1/52

Probability Problem: Drawing Two Cards from a Deck

Solution:

Suppose we take two cards from a well-shuffled deck of playing cards, one after the other.

Without Replacement:

What will be the probability that we will see two red cards?

Probability = \( \frac{26}{52} \times \frac{25}{51} \)

With Replacement:

What will be the probability that we will see two red cards?

Probability = \( \frac{26}{52} \times \frac{26}{52} \)

Change in Probability with Replacement:

When we repeat the experiment with replacement, the probability of seeing two reds remains the same for each draw. This is because each draw is independent of the previous one, and replacing the card does not change the composition of the deck.

Therefore, the probability of seeing two reds in successive draws with replacement remains \( \left(\frac{26}{52}\right)^2 \), or \( \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \).

In mutually exclusive events, if event A happens, event B cannot happen.

Random Variables and Probability

Random variables are a fundamental concept in statistics and probability theory. They represent numerical outcomes of a random phenomenon, such as the result of rolling a die or flipping a coin.

Coin Flip Example

Consider the example of flipping a coin. If you flip a coin 10 times, you might wonder how many times you will get heads. This is where the concept of a random variable comes into play.

While you might expect to get heads 5 times (half of the flips), it's not guaranteed. Each flip is independent of the others, so the actual number of heads could vary.

Expected Value of a Random Variable

The expected value of a random variable is a measure of its central tendency. It represents the average outcome over many repetitions of the experiment.

For example, if you were to flip a coin 100 times, the expected number of heads can be calculated by summing the products of each possible outcome (0 through 100) with its probability.

In the case of 10 coin flips, the expected value might not be a whole number, indicating that you're likely to get a fractional or decimal result on average.

Distribution of Outcomes

When dealing with random variables, it's essential to understand the distribution of possible outcomes. This distribution describes the likelihood of each outcome occurring.

For example, if we were to collect data on the number of heads obtained from flipping a coin 10 times over many trials, we would observe a distribution of outcomes. Some outcomes might be more likely than others.

In practical scenarios, such as the example of flight no-shows, the distribution of outcomes can help predict the likelihood of certain events, such as the percentage of flights with no-show passengers.

Probability Distribution

A probability distribution is a graphical representation of the possible outcomes of a random variable and their associated probabilities.

The x-axis of the distribution represents all possible outcomes of the random variable, while the y-axis represents the probability of each outcome occurring.

Probability distribution charts provide a visual way to understand the likelihood of different outcomes and are crucial tools in statistical analysis.

Probability Distribution Functions

A probability distribution encompasses all potential outcomes of an experiment performed multiple times. It provides a way to understand the likelihood of different results occurring.

Discrete Probability Distribution

In a discrete probability distribution, the outcomes are distinct and listed individually. For example, when rolling a six-sided die, the possible outcomes are discrete (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6). Each outcome has an associated probability of occurring, and the sum of all probabilities equals 1.

Continuous Probability Distribution

In contrast, a continuous probability distribution arises from experiments with continuous possible outcomes. These outcomes form an uncountable set, often representing a range of values rather than distinct points. For example, the heights of individuals in a population or the time taken for a process to complete can be continuous variables. Probability density functions are used to describe the likelihood of observing values within a range.

Key Concepts

  • Sample Space: The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  • Probability Mass Function (PMF): For discrete distributions, the PMF assigns probabilities to each possible outcome.
  • Probability Density Function (PDF): For continuous distributions, the PDF describes the relative likelihood of different values within a range.

Understanding probability distribution functions is essential for analyzing data, making predictions, and drawing conclusions in various fields such as statistics, engineering, finance, and natural sciences.

Probability Distribution Functions

A probability distribution function provides insights into the likelihood of different outcomes occurring in a random experiment. Two common concepts in probability theory are the Probability Distribution Function (PDF) and the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF).

Probability Distribution Function (PDF)

The Probability Distribution Function (PDF) is a depiction of all possible outcomes and their associated probabilities in a probability distribution. For discrete random variables, the PDF assigns probabilities to each individual outcome, while for continuous random variables, it describes the relative likelihood of different values within a range.

Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) provides a cumulative view of the probabilities associated with a random variable. It represents the probability that the outcome of a random variable is less than or equal to a specific value. Mathematically, the CDF for an outcome \( x \) (denoted as \( CDF(x) \)) is calculated as the probability of all outcomes less than or equal to \( x \):

\( CDF(x) = P(X \leq x) \)

Very often, we are interested in the cumulative probabilities of outcomes less than or equal to a certain value rather than the probability of a specific outcome itself. The CDF allows us to understand the cumulative likelihood of observing values up to a given point in the distribution.
