Binomial Distribution Tutorial

Welcome to our video on binomial distribution! In this tutorial, you will learn about the definition of binomial distribution, see an example illustrating binomial distribution, and understand how to transform binomial distribution to a standard normal distribution.

1. Definition of Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution arises from a random experiment with two possible outcomes, success (denoted by \( p \)) and failure (denoted by \( q \)). If \( x \) represents the number of successes in \( n \) independent trials, then the probability mass function of the binomial distribution is given by:

\[ P(X = x) = \binom{n}{x} \cdot p^x \cdot q^{n-x} \]

where \( \binom{n}{x} \) represents the number of ways \( x \) successes can occur in \( n \) trials.

2. Example of Binomial Distribution

Consider a situation where the chance of winning an election in a constituency by an independent candidate is 0.31. In a by-election to an assembly constituency in Karnataka, if five independent candidates have contested, we can use binomial distribution to find the probability of an independent candidate winning the election.

3. Transformation to Standard Normal Distribution

To transform a binomial distribution to a standard normal distribution, we define the random variable \( Z \) as:

\[ Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \]

where \( X \) is the number of successes, \( \mu \) is the mean of the binomial distribution, and \( \sigma \) is the standard deviation. This transformation allows us to work with the standard normal distribution, which has a mean of zero and a variance of one.

Summary

In this video, you learned about the concept of binomial distribution, saw an example illustrating its application, and understood how to transform it into a standard normal distribution for easier analysis.
