Definition of Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' Theorem is a fundamental principle in probability theory that allows us to update the probability of an event based on new evidence. Mathematically, it is represented as:

P(K|B) = [P(B|K) * P(K)] / Î£[P(B|K) * P(K)]

Example: Biscuit Packing

In a biscuit factory, three workers pack biscuits with varying probabilities of breakage. Given the probability of broken biscuits for each worker, we can calculate the posterior probability of a biscuit being packed by a specific worker.

Mathematical Definition

Bayes' Theorem mathematically defines the posterior probability (P(K|B)) given a common event B and prior probabilities (P(K)) for disjoint events. It involves calculating the likelihood (P(B|K)) and evidence (Î£[P(B|K) * P(K)]).

Additional Examples

Bayes' Theorem can be applied in various scenarios, such as determining the likelihood of a farmer having taken a loan given that they have committed suicide, or calculating the probability of a new policyholder having an accident within one year.
